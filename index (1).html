Your next step

  1. You start here

    One simple blood test, every 2–3 years

    Get your cholesterol (ask the lab for a "lipid profile") and blood sugar ("HbA1c") checked, plus a BP reading. One visit to any lab before breakfast, under ₹800, results the same day.

  2. You start here

    A proper yearly check-up

    The same blood tests, but every year, plus a BP check and a basic heart tracing (ECG). One rule: sit with a doctor and discuss the report — don't just collect the PDF and file it away.

  3. You start here

    Yearly check-up + a 10-minute heart scan

    Along with the yearly tests, ask your doctor about a "CT calcium score" — a quick scan, no needles in your arteries, that shows if plaque has already started building. That's the thing blood tests and ECG simply can't see. Around ₹3,000–8,000. Based on the score, your doctor decides if anything more is needed.

  4. See a doctor first

    Feeling symptoms? That changes everything

    Forget the health package for now — see a doctor this week and describe exactly what you're feeling. Everything else on this list is for people who feel completely fine.

  5. You're here

    Already a heart patient? Your path is different

    After a stent, bypass or heart attack, this checklist no longer applies to you — your cardiologist's plan does. Take the medicines daily, keep every review appointment, and treat any new chest symptom as an emergency, not a "let's see".

What each test actually does

Because "I had an ECG, I'm fine" is how people get surprised.

Lipid profile (LDL, HDL, cholesterol)
ScreeningMeasures the raw material of blockages. Catches: your long-term risk. Misses: whether plaque has already formed. Normal lipids today don't erase 20 years of bad lipids.
Resting ECG
ScreeningRecords the heart's electrical rhythm for a few seconds at rest. Catches: rhythm problems, signs of an old heart attack. Misses: most blockages. An artery can be 90% blocked with a completely normal resting ECG — this is exactly what happened in the story that started this. ECG ≠ blockage test.
TMT / Stress test (treadmill)
ScreeningECG while you exercise — blockages that hide at rest often show under load. Catches: blockages big enough to limit blood flow (typically >70%). Misses: smaller plaques, and it has false alarms. Useful, but not the final word.
CT Coronary Calcium Score
ScreeningA quick CT scan that counts calcified plaque in your coronary arteries. Score 0 = very low risk for years; higher scores = plaque is already there, act now. This is usually the test the "should I worry?" question is really asking for. Non-invasive, no injection, ~10 minutes.
CT Coronary Angiography
DiagnosticCT scan with dye injected into a vein — shows the arteries and blockages in detail without surgery. Often the next step after a high calcium score or suspicious symptoms. One limit: heavy calcification makes it overestimate narrowing (false alarms), and movement or a fast heart rate blurs it. That's why doctors usually do the calcium score first — it tells them whether a CT angio will even be readable, or whether a stress test / invasive angio is the smarter next step. The order of tests is the doctor's craft; don't self-order this one.
Invasive (catheter) angiography
ProcedureA tube goes from your wrist/groin into the heart's arteries. The gold standard — and often the same sitting where a stent is placed. But it's not a screening test. It carries small but real risks, so doctors order it when other tests or symptoms point to a problem, not as an annual checkup. If someone tells you "everyone over 40 should just get an angiography," they mean well, but the non-invasive CT options above are the right starting point.

Why doctors don't hunt every blockage

The most counterintuitive — and most important — fact on this page.

If everyone in our group got an angiogram tomorrow, most of us would show some plaque — that's normal for our age. So "do I have a blockage?" is the wrong question. Here's why:

Most heart attacks don't come from the big blockages. They come from smaller plaques (30–50%) that suddenly crack open and form a clot — plaques too small to cause symptoms, too small to fail a stress test, too small to "need" a stent. That's how someone passes a checkup and collapses months later.

This is also why stents don't prevent heart attacks in stable people. Large trials have shown that stenting a stable blockage relieves symptoms but doesn't reduce heart attacks or death compared to medicines alone. Stents save lives during an attack. What prevents one is different: statins and BP control, which lower LDL enough that plaques harden, stabilise, and stop rupturing — every plaque at once, including the ones no scan flagged.

So the real purpose of testing is calibration, not treasure-hunting. A calcium score doesn't map your blockages — it tells you and your doctor how aggressively to fight: lifestyle alone, or lifestyle plus medicines, and at what intensity. Finding plaque isn't the failure. Not acting on your risk is.

If someone collapses

  1. Tap & shout. No response, no normal breathing? Assume cardiac arrest.
  2. Point at one person: "You — call 108 and find an AED."
  3. Push hard and fast on the centre of the chest — 100–120 pushes per minute, about 5 cm deep. Don't stop until help arrives. Hands-only is enough.
  4. Use the AED the moment it arrives — it talks you through everything.

Every minute without CPR cuts survival by ~10%. A half-day course makes you the reason someone's kids still have a father. Ask in the group — we're organising one.
