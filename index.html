Your next step

  1. You start here

    Tier 1 · Baseline numbers, every 2–3 years

    Lipid profile + HbA1c (blood sugar) + BP reading. Cheap, fasting blood test, any lab.

  2. You start here

    Tier 2 · Annual panel + resting ECG

    Same tests, every year, plus an ECG as a baseline. Discuss results with a physician, not just the lab report.

  3. You start here

    Tier 3 · Ask about a CT Coronary Calcium Score

    A 10-minute non-invasive scan that directly measures plaque in your arteries — the thing ECG and lipid tests can't see. Roughly ₹3,000–8,000. Your doctor may add a TMT or CT angiography based on the score.

  4. See a doctor first

    Symptoms? Skip screening — see a doctor now

    Screening tests are for people who feel fine. Symptoms need proper evaluation this week, not an annual package.

  5. You're here

    Known heart disease · Maintenance, not screening

    Stent, bypass, or a past heart attack means the screening ladder no longer applies — your cardiologist's follow-up plan does. Medicines, targets, and reviews are the whole game now.

What each test actually does

Because "I had an ECG, I'm fine" is how people get surprised.

Lipid profile (LDL, HDL, cholesterol)
ScreeningMeasures the raw material of blockages. Catches: your long-term risk. Misses: whether plaque has already formed. Normal lipids today don't erase 20 years of bad lipids.
Resting ECG
ScreeningRecords the heart's electrical rhythm for a few seconds at rest. Catches: rhythm problems, signs of an old heart attack. Misses: most blockages. An artery can be 90% blocked with a completely normal resting ECG — this is exactly what happened in the story that started this. ECG ≠ blockage test.
TMT / Stress test (treadmill)
ScreeningECG while you exercise — blockages that hide at rest often show under load. Catches: blockages big enough to limit blood flow (typically >70%). Misses: smaller plaques, and it has false alarms. Useful, but not the final word.
CT Coronary Calcium Score
ScreeningA quick CT scan that counts calcified plaque in your coronary arteries. Score 0 = very low risk for years; higher scores = plaque is already there, act now. This is usually the test the "should I worry?" question is really asking for. Non-invasive, no injection, ~10 minutes.
CT Coronary Angiography
DiagnosticCT scan with dye injected into a vein — shows the arteries and blockages in detail without surgery. Often the next step after a high calcium score or suspicious symptoms.
Invasive (catheter) angiography
ProcedureA tube goes from your wrist/groin into the heart's arteries. The gold standard — and often the same sitting where a stent is placed. But it's not a screening test. It carries small but real risks, so doctors order it when other tests or symptoms point to a problem, not as an annual checkup. If someone tells you "everyone over 40 should just get an angiography," they mean well, but the non-invasive CT options above are the right starting point.

If someone collapses

  1. Tap & shout. No response, no normal breathing? Assume cardiac arrest.
  2. Point at one person: "You — call 108 and find an AED."
  3. Push hard and fast on the centre of the chest — 100–120 pushes per minute, about 5 cm deep. Don't stop until help arrives. Hands-only is enough.
  4. Use the AED the moment it arrives — it talks you through everything.

Every minute without CPR cuts survival by ~10%. A half-day course makes you the reason someone's kids still have a father. Ask in the group — we're organising one.
